# Kilroy — Brand Playbook

> **Read before writing any Kilroy brief.** This playbook encodes the
> rules that make a Kilroy piece feel authentic. If a choice conflicts
> with this playbook, the playbook wins.

## The Two-Layer Rule

Every Kilroy piece has exactly two layers. Get this wrong and the piece
is not Kilroy.

**Layer 1 — THE SCENE.** A real-looking photograph. Documentary-grade.
Could plausibly have appeared in a newspaper archive, a family album,
a military photo library, a crypto explorer screenshot. No AI tells.
Named lens, named film stock or sensor, named lighting condition.

**Layer 2 — THE TAG.** A small chalk-or-sharpie drawing of the Kilroy
peek figure (bald head, nose, four fingers over a wall) with "KILROY
WAS HERE" hand-lettered below. Placed in a corner. Never more than
~15% of the frame area.

Layer 1 looks found. Layer 2 looks drawn by a GI with nothing to do.

## The Character (invariants)

1. **Bald crown, no eyebrows, no hair.** A few stray hairs are
   acceptable in the 1944-accurate variants; do not embellish.
2. **Long drooping nose.** Extends down PAST the wall / edge Kilroy is
   peeking over. This is the single most identifying feature.
3. **Eyes are two simple dots or small ovals.** No whites, no pupils,
   no emotion. Kilroy does not react.
4. **Four fingers gripping the top of the wall.** Not five. Always
   drawn as four short vertical lines with a curve underneath.
5. **ONE continuous line.** The whole drawing reads as a single gesture
   — a bored soldier's doodle. No cross-hatching, no shading, no fill.

## Placement library

Choose ONE per piece. Never stack.

- **Bottom-right corner** (most common — GI habit)
- **Top-left, on a wall in the background** of a street photo
- **On a chalkboard / whiteboard** in a corporate or lab scene
- **On a stall door** in a bathroom scene (Potsdam reference)
- **On a helmet / rifle stock** in a military scene
- **On a tombstone / headstone** in a graveyard frame
- **On a ship's hull** (Fore River canon reference)
- **In the lower margin of a historic newspaper page**
- **Scratched into the paint** of a vintage car door
- **On the side of a sealed cargo container**

## When Kilroy works (earn it)

- Retrospective moments: a famous market event, in hindsight, where
  you can credibly claim Kilroy saw it coming.
- Unglamorous locations: the back office, the rug-pull aftermath,
  the shipyard, the bathroom, the barracks. Kilroy does NOT belong in
  glossy hero shots.
- Real news photos given a Kilroy tag: this is the single highest-
  conversion format. Pick a well-known news photo (NASA moon landing,
  Trump 1987, Reagan/Gorbachev, Lehman brothers 2008) and tag it.

## When Kilroy breaks (kill list)

- Kilroy as the subject of the frame. He is NEVER the subject.
- Kilroy smiling, winking, or making a face. He is blank.
- Kilroy in color. The tag is always monochrome chalk or sharpie.
- A polished / vector / perfect Kilroy. Must look hand-drawn.
- Kilroy paired with explainer caption ("Kilroy knew"). The tag works
  BECAUSE viewers recognize it without context. Explaining kills it.
- Kilroy in pure AI-slop territory: flying, posing, chibi-fied, in a
  group shot with other memes. He is a graffiti mark, not a mascot.

## Prompt scaffolds

### Kilroy tags a real photo (PRIMARY FORMAT)

```
<NEWS-PHOTO-STYLE DESCRIPTION>: [subject], [year], [photographer or
camera], [lighting condition], [film stock or press-flash look],
[aspect]. In the [placement: bottom-right corner / background wall /
etc.], a chalk graffiti: a bald head with a long drooping nose and
four fingers gripping the edge of [surface], eyes peeking over. Below
the figure, hand-lettered block caps: "KILROY WAS HERE". The chalk
graffiti is pure white, uneven, slightly smudged — obviously drawn
by hand. The rest of the frame looks like a real archival press photo.
No lens flares unless specified. No AI-faces. No retouching.
```

### Kilroy tags a crypto moment

```
Cold trading terminal at 3am: [Hyperliquid / TradingView / Discord
screenshot specifics], [exact chart pattern], bar showing [named
candle]. In the lower-right of the monitor bezel, a chalk-drawn
Kilroy peek figure with "KILROY WAS HERE" hand-lettered below. The
terminal looks pixel-accurate; the Kilroy is obvious marker graffiti
on the physical monitor. Aspect: 16:9 monitor, photographed at a
slight angle, warm tungsten room light, keyboard in frame.
```

### Kilroy on a wall behind a historic scene

```
Wide shot, [named historical scene — D-Day landing craft approaching
Omaha / NASA Mission Control during Apollo 11 / Paul Volcker's office
1979]. In the background, on [a concrete wall / a stall door / a crate
in the corner], a small chalk Kilroy peek with "KILROY WAS HERE"
lettered below. The scene itself is the subject — Kilroy is a 6% detail
in the lower third. Period-accurate, press-photo grain, 35mm Tri-X
pushed one stop, natural light.
```

## Cross-pollination with other Kilroy variants

The global family:
- **Mr Chad (UK)** — curling hair like a question mark, crossed-eyes, paired with "Wot no [shortage]?"
- **Smoe / Clem (Australia)**
- **Vasya (Russia)** — "Vasya was here"
- **Sapo (Chile)** — "nosy person"
- **Józef Tkaczuk (Poland)**

For this brand, use **Kilroy** as the default. If a piece specifically
riffs on British rationing humor or a Russia-themed moment, you MAY
use the regional variant — but the character drawing is the same; only
the legend changes.

## The don't-explain rule

If you are tempted to write a caption like *"Kilroy was there for the
FTX collapse"* — don't. The graffiti IS the claim. The caption should
be about the SCENE, not about Kilroy. Let viewers discover the tag on
their own. That moment of discovery is the entire compounding mechanic
of this brand.

## One-line summary

*Kilroy is the signature, not the subject. The photo is real, the
graffiti is chalk, and the joke is that he got there first.*
