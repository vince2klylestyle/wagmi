# Kilroy — Style Codex

> **Tagline:** *Kilroy was here.*
>
> **Brand thesis:** Kilroy is an 80-year-old global graffiti meme. The
> character is a bald head with a long drooping nose and four fingers
> gripping the top of a wall, with "KILROY WAS HERE" hand-lettered
> underneath. He is never the subject — he is the signature left behind.
> We piggyback on a pre-existing, universally recognized visual meme and
> point it at crypto / internet / market moments.

## Historical canon (cite this — the authenticity comes from the lineage)

- **Origin (likely):** James J. Kilroy, Fore River shipyard inspector,
  Quincy MA, 1941. Marked riveter inspections in crayon so workers
  couldn't erase and re-charge for the work. The marks shipped out
  inside sealed hull compartments on WWII troopships — GIs saw them
  everywhere they went and started replicating.
- **British variant:** "Mr Chad" with a curling question-mark hair and
  crossed-eyes — predates or parallels Kilroy. Often paired with "Wot, no
  [thing]?" rationing complaints.
- **Other names worth knowing:** Smoe, Clem (Australia), Józef Tkaczuk
  (Poland), Sapo (Chile), Vasya (Russia). The visual is the same — only
  the signature legend changes.
- **The legend:** Stalin found a Kilroy graffito in the VIP bathroom at
  the Potsdam Conference and asked his staff who Kilroy was.
  Nazi intelligence reportedly believed Kilroy was a code-name for a
  top-tier Allied spy.
- **Mundane landmarks:** Kilroy appeared on the atomic bomb test site at
  Bikini Atoll (1946), on the Berlin Wall, on the moon in a 1948 Bugs
  Bunny cartoon, and is engraved on the WWII Memorial in Washington DC.
- **Kilroy's meaning:** the everyman who was always there first. The
  nobody who witnessed the biggest events. The graffiti democracy of
  enlisted men stamping their presence on history.

## Visual rules

### The character (NEVER violate)

1. **Only the top of the head and fingers show.** Bald crown, eyes,
   long drooping nose, four knuckles gripping the top of a wall /
   frame / chart / terminal. Never a full body. Never shoulders.
2. **Drawn with ONE continuous chalk or sharpie line.** No shading, no
   color, no gradients. White on dark, or black on light.
3. **Always paired with "KILROY WAS HERE"** hand-lettered in block caps
   directly below, slightly rough. The handwriting matters — stencil
   or a clean font kills the GI-crayon authenticity.
4. **Placement is always a corner / edge.** Kilroy is graffiti. He is
   not the subject of the frame. He is the signature on the wall of
   someone else's picture.

### The frame (the actual photograph Kilroy tags)

- Real-looking news or historical photograph as the base layer: D-Day
  landing craft, ENIAC computer room, 1987 Trump at Trump Tower, a
  cold trading terminal at 3am, a burnt-out exchange office.
- Or: a crypto-native scene — a chart breakdown, a dry Discord text
  channel screenshot, a Uniswap swap receipt, a Solana explorer tx.
- Base image should look REAL. The Kilroy on top is always obviously
  graffiti — chalk, sharpie, spray stencil.

### Color

- Kilroy himself: **pure white chalk** on dark surfaces, **pure black
  sharpie** on light surfaces. Never tinted.
- The photograph underneath: documentary / photojournalism palette —
  muted sepia, press-flash, concrete grey, olive, chalk dust.

### Typography

- "KILROY WAS HERE" — hand-lettered block caps, slightly uneven.
- Any other text on the piece (caption, headline, ticker): **always a
  separate register from the graffiti**. Newspaper serif, teletype
  monospace, or tabloid slab. Never match the graffiti font.

## What this brand DOES

- Piggybacks on 80 years of universal recognition. Most humans on Earth
  have seen this character; they don't know why it's funny, but they
  know it.
- Positions the brand as the omnipresent witness. "Kilroy was here when
  [FTX collapsed / ETH launched / you bought the top / Solana hit $300]."
- Uses anachronism: 1944 GI graffiti tagging a 2026 crypto moment. The
  gap IS the joke.
- Carries lore for free. Every piece is a contribution to a 80-year
  tradition, not a new thing we have to explain.

## What this brand DOES NOT do

- Show Kilroy's full body. He is ONLY a peek.
- Smile. Kilroy's face is blank — just nose and eyes.
- Explain. If the caption reads "Kilroy saw this coming," the piece
  fails. The graffiti does the work.
- Drift into Pepe / Wojak territory. Different meme lineages.
- Read as AI slop. The base photo looks found, the graffiti looks
  drawn by a bored GI in 1944.
